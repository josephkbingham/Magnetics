Heating vs. Saturation, One Knob at a Time

Every inductor design decision pushes on two gauges: peak flux density B̂ (how close the core is to saturating) and temperature rise ΔT (how hot the losses make it). This page takes one worked baseline design, sweeps each design variable while holding everything else fixed, and plots what happens to both gauges. The dot on every curve marks the baseline design; dashed red lines are the limits.

Saturation gauge

Peak flux at the worst-case instantaneous current must stay under the material limit.

B̂ = L ÎN Ac ≈ μ0 N Îℓg + ℓc/μr ≤ Bmax

Saturation cares about the peak current, the turns, the core area, and the gap — nothing else.

Heating gauge

Copper loss plus core loss, times the thermal resistance of the finished part.

ΔT = Rθ (Pcu + Pfe)
Pcu = Irms2R,  Pfe = Kc fα Bacβ Vc,  Rθ ≈ 0.06√Vc

Heating cares about the RMS current, wire resistance, flux ripple, frequency, and body size.

Baseline design — a 100 kHz buck output choke
N

Turns N — the fundamental trade

Held constant: L (the gap is re-cut for each N), core, wire always refilled to the full window.

With the inductance pinned at 47 µH, more turns means less flux per amp but more, thinner wire in the same window. The two gauges move in opposite directions — this is the central tension of inductor design:

B̂ = L ÎN Ac flux falls as 1/N
Pcu = Irms2 ρw · MLT · N2ku Wa wire area shrinks as 1/N, length grows as N → R ∝ N²
B̂ ∝ 1/N ▼ safer Pcu ∝ N2 ▲ hotter Pfe ∝ 1/Nβ ▼ ℓg ∝ N2

Turns set where you sit between the two failure modes. Too few turns and the core saturates; too many and the copper cooks. The feasible window here is roughly N ≈ 17–31 — and the required gap grows as N2, which is why high-turn designs need impractically large (fringing-heavy) gaps.

ℓg

Air gap ℓg — the anti-saturation knob

Held constant: N = 20, core, wire, volt-seconds from the converter. L is allowed to follow the gap.

Opening the gap lowers inductance and flux per amp together. The DC flux falls almost in proportion to 1/ℓg — but the lost inductance comes back as current ripple. Note the surprise in the third panel: heating barely moves, because flux ripple is set by the converter's volt-seconds (ΔB = V·ton/N Ac), not by the gap.

L = μ0 N2 Acℓg + ℓc/μr , B̂ = μ0 N Îℓg + ℓc/μr both ∝ 1/ℓg once the gap dominates
B̂ ≈ ∝ 1/ℓg ▼ safer L ≈ ∝ 1/ℓg ▲ ripple grows ΔB unchanged volt-second driven ΔT ≈ flat only ripple RMS adds

The gap trades inductance for saturation margin at almost no thermal cost. That's why gapping (or a distributed-gap powder core) is the standard fix for DC-biased chokes. The price is paid elsewhere: more ripple current stresses the output capacitor and switches, and very large gaps fringe.

a

Core size (scale a) — the knob that fixes both

Held constant: N = 20, L (re-gapped), window kept full. Every linear dimension scales by a: Ac, Wa ∝ a2, Vc ∝ a3.

Scaling the whole core up gives flux more iron and copper more window and the losses more surface to escape from. It is the only knob that improves both gauges at once — which is exactly why the area product Ap = AcWa ∝ a4 works as a one-number core-size selector.

B̂ ∝ 1/a2 ▼ safer Pcu ∝ 1/a ▼ Rθ ∝ a−1.5 ▼ ΔT ≈ ∝ a−2.5 ▼ cooler cost & volume ∝ a3 ▲

When both gauges are in the red, stop tuning and pick a bigger core. Everything improves steeply with size — the only things that grow are volume, mass, and cost, which is the actual engineering trade.

J

Wire size (current density J) — a pure heating knob

Held constant: N = 20, L, core, gap. Wire cross-section Aw = Irms/J varies.

Thinner wire = higher current density = more resistance = more heat, in a straight line. And saturation does not care at all — flux depends on amps and turns, not on how thick the copper carrying those amps is. The two gauges fully decouple here:

Pcu = Irms2 ρw N · MLTAw = ρw N · MLT · Irms J loss is linear in J
Pcu ∝ J ▲ hotter B̂ unchanged — flat line fit limit: J ≥ IrmsN/kuWa

Wire gauge buys temperature, never saturation margin. Use the fattest wire that fits the window (the shaded region is physically unbuildable — the copper won't fit). Typical designs land at 3–6 A/mm²; the thermally-derived Jo equation in the area-product method is just a formal way of picking this point.

f

Switching frequency f — set upstream, felt here

Held constant: L, N, gap, core, wire. Converter volt-seconds shrink as 1/f, so ripple ΔI = ΔI0·f0/f.

With the same inductor, running the converter faster shrinks the current and flux ripple (ΔB ∝ 1/f). Core loss scales as fαΔBβ ∝ f α−β — and since β > α for ferrite (here 2.75 vs 1.46), core loss falls with frequency at fixed L. The catch is the skin effect: current crowds into a skin depth δ ∝ 1/√f, raising AC resistance — small here because the current is mostly DC, dominant in transformers.

ΔI, ΔB ∝ 1/f ▼ Î ↓ slightly ▼ safer Pfe ∝ f α−β ▼ Rac/Rdc ↑ ▲ (ripple only)

At fixed L, faster switching relaxes this inductor — less ripple, less peak flux, less core loss. In a real design you'd cash that in by reducing L (∝ 1/f) and shrinking the core, which is the true reason converters keep moving up in frequency. The bill goes to the switches (switching loss) and to AC winding loss.
